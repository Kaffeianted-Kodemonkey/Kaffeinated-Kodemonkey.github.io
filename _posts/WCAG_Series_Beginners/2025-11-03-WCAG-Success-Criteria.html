Why WCAG Success Criteria Matter

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are the gold standard for making digital content accessible to everyone, including people with disabilities. At the heart of WCAG are its success criteria, specific requirements that ensure websites are Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust (POUR). This post dives into what WCAG success criteria are, how they align with POUR principles, and practical steps to meet them, ensuring your website is inclusive and compliant with global standards like WCAG 2.2 and U.S.-specific requirements like Section 508.

Whether you’re a developer, designer, or federal contractor, understanding and applying these criteria will help you create websites that work for all users, from screen reader users to those navigating by keyboard.

What Are WCAG Success Criteria?

WCAG success criteria are measurable standards that define how to make web content accessible. Organized under the POUR principles, they’re split into three conformance levels:

  • Level A: Basic accessibility (e.g., alt text for images).
  • Level AA: Addresses common barriers (e.g., sufficient color contrast).
  • Level AAA: Highest accessibility, often for specialized audiences.

For example, WCAG 2.2 (ISO/IEC 40500, October 2025) includes 86 success criteria, with Level A criteria like 1.1.1 (non-text content) requiring alt text for images so screen readers can describe visuals. These criteria are globally recognized, translated into over 20 languages, and form the backbone of accessibility laws in regions like the EU, Canada, and the U.S. (where 26% of adults have disabilities, per CDC).

Section 508 and POUR

In the U.S., Section 508 (updated 2017, part of the Rehabilitation Act) requires federal websites and ICT (e.g., PDFs, software) to meet WCAG 2.0 Level A/AA success criteria. For example, federal contractors must ensure alt text (1.1.1) and captions (1.2.2) are in place and document compliance via Voluntary Product Accessibility Templates (VPATs). While WCAG’s POUR-based criteria apply globally, Section 508 narrows the focus to U.S. federal projects, making it a legal requirement for government work.

Conclusion

WCAG’s global reach makes it the foundation for accessible web design, with Level A criteria like alt text ensuring inclusivity worldwide. Section 508, focused on U.S. federal ICT, aligns with WCAG 2.0 Level A/AA to meet legal requirements. By integrating these standards, you can create websites that serve diverse audiences and comply with federal mandates.
